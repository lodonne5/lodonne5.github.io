Introduction¶

I have chosen to use the data set called "US Cars Dataset" from https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/doaaalsenani/usa-cers-dataset. This data set contains plenty of quantitative data about the cars which I feel will be better suited for a machine learning related project. It also has more features and the information was provided by AUCTION EXPORT.com.

The big question I wish to answer about my dataset is: Which feature of a car predicts whether a car is cheap?

Statistical Method 1: ANOVA test

I would like to know whether there is a correlation between the vehicle's color and its mileage on the auction website for colors with car mileages greater than 100000.

  • H0: There is no correlation between a vehicle's color and its mileage.
  • H: There is a correlation between a vehicle's color and its mileage.
In [ ]:
color_lst = df['color'].unique().tolist()
avg_mileages = []
colors = []

for val in color_lst:
  if df['mileage'][df['color'] == val].mean() >= 100000:
    colors.append(val)

for color in colors:
  avg_mileages.append(df['mileage'][df['color'] == color].mean())

I believe an ANOVA test for colors with means greater than 100,000 would be most appropriate here as I want to compare the means of over 3 different groups of cars as there are many car colors.

In [ ]:
gold_samp = df['mileage'][df["color"] == 'gold']

Since the P-value is greater than a = 0.05, I should not reject the null hypothesis and thus there is no correlation between a car's color and its mileage.

From the graph, it appears that dark blue and burgundy have the greatest average mileage.

Statistical Method 3: Two Sample T-test

Finally, I would like to know if a car being silver is correlated with its price on the auction website.

  • H0: A car being silver is not correlated with its price.
  • H: A car being silver is correlated with its price.

I feel that a two-sample T-test should be sufficient for establishing whether there is a correlation as I am comparing two groups: silver and not silver and the other variable is quantitative. I should put the prices for the silver cars in one list and the prices for the non-silver cars in another list.

In [ ]:
silver_df = df[df['color'] == 'silver']
not_silver_df = df[df['color'] != 'silver']
result = scipy.stats.ttest_ind(silver_df['price'], not_silver_df['price'])
print("P-value: " + str(result.pvalue))

P-value: 0.05136574230380793

Since the p value is greater than the alpha value of 0.05, I should not reject the null hypothesis and therefore, there is no correlation between a car being silver or not and its price.

Primary analysis¶

In this section, we will be using machine learning to attempt to find the best predictors of car price on the car auction website. We would like to solve the big question brought up in the introduction which is: What features of a car on this auction website is the best predictor of whether a car is cheap?

I have heard that random forest supervised machine learning models have very high accuracy for testing data as it is an ensemble of decision trees in which the decision trees that have overfitting are outvoted by the better decision trees. Decision trees on the other hand are more likely to overfit. We will compare the two machine learning models below in terms of accuracy and F1 score.
